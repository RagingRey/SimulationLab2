## Part 2: Motion Physics and Collision Response

### 2.1 Motion Physics Integration

The motion of rigid bodies is evaluated discretely every simulation tick within `PhysicsObject::Update`. For objects with a finite mass (where `m_InverseMass > 0.0f`), the engine accumulates external forces, most notably applying a continuous gravitational force vector: `{0.0f, gravity * m_Mass, 0.0f}`.

The engine supports toggling between two numerical integration schemes at runtime via the `IntegrationMethod` enum:

**1. Explicit Euler:** The position is updated using the current velocity, and then the velocity is updated using the acceleration:

$$x_{t+1} = x_t + v_t \Delta t$$

$$v_{t+1} = v_t + a \Delta t$$

**2. Semi-Implicit Euler:** The velocity is updated first, and the position is updated using the new velocity:

$$v_{t+1} = v_t + a \Delta t$$

$$x_{t+1} = x_t + v_{t+1} \Delta t$$

Semi-Implicit Euler is the default and preferred method because it preserves energy much better over time, maintaining the stability of the simulation — especially for stacked objects or those under constant acceleration.

Angular motion is handled in `IntegrateAngularVelocity`. The angular velocity vector (in radians per second) is converted into a delta quaternion representing the rotation over `deltaTime`. This is multiplied by the object's current orientation quaternion (`SetOrientation(current * delta)`) to apply the rotation without succumbing to gimbal lock.

### 2.2 Collision Detection (Narrow-Phase)

The `SimCollision` namespace handles the narrow-phase intersection tests, returning a `Contact` struct containing the boolean hit state, the penetration depth, and the collision normal. To ensure mathematical stability, utility functions like `SafeNormalize` and `OrthonormalizeColumns` are utilised to prevent division by zero and correct matrix drift resulting from floating-point inaccuracies.

- **Sphere Collisions (`SphereVsSphere` / `SphereVsPlane`):** These rely on straightforward distance calculations. For a plane, the distance from the sphere's center to the plane is evaluated using the dot product against the plane's normal.

- **OBB vs OBB (Cuboids):** The engine uses the Separating Axis Theorem (SAT) for Oriented Bounding Boxes. It tests 15 potential separating axes: 3 local axes from Box A, 3 from Box B, and 9 cross products of their axes. The algorithm calculates the projected radii (`ra` and `rb`) and the translation distance along each axis. If an overlap is found on all 15 axes, a collision is registered, and the axis with the minimum overlap becomes the collision normal.

- **Sphere vs OBB:** The sphere's center is transformed into the local space of the OBB via the transposed orientation matrix. It is then clamped to the box's half-extents to find the closest point on the box. If the squared distance to this point is less than the sphere's squared radius, a collision occurs.

### 2.3 Collision Response

When a collision is registered, `PhysicsObject::ResolveCollision` applies a two-step mathematical response to simulate realistic physics while preserving engine stability.

**1. Positional Correction:** If objects are allowed to intersect deeply, the integration step can cause them to gain massive amounts of artificial energy, resulting in "explosions." To prevent this, a non-physical positional correction is applied before velocity impulses. The objects are pushed apart along the collision normal by a fraction (`percent = 0.8f`) of the penetration depth, minus a slop threshold (`0.01f`) to prevent micro-jitter when resting. The push is distributed based on the objects' inverse masses.

**2. Impulse Resolution (Linear and Angular):** The engine calculates the relative velocity at the exact point of contact. This accounts for both linear velocity and the velocity induced by the objects' rotation, calculated via the cross product of the angular velocity and the radius vector from the center of mass to the contact point (`velA = GetVelocity() + glm::cross(GetAngularVelocity(), rA)`).

If the objects are moving apart (`velAlongNormal > 0.0f`), the resolution aborts. Otherwise, the engine calculates a scalar impulse $j$. This calculation uses the combined coefficient of restitution $e$ (bounciness) and factors in rotational resistance using the inverse inertia tensors ($I^{-1}$) of both objects. The formula implemented in the code is:

$$j = \frac{-(1 + e) \cdot v_{rel} \cdot n}{m_{invA} + m_{invB} + n \cdot \left((I^{-1}_A (r_A \times n)) \times r_A + (I^{-1}_B (r_B \times n)) \times r_B\right)}$$

The final impulse vector ($j \cdot n$) is then applied to the linear velocity. Simultaneously, an angular impulse is generated by crossing the radius vector with the impulse vector and multiplying by the inverse inertia tensor, which realistically alters the object's spin upon impact.
